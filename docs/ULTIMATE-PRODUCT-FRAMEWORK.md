# Celestual: The Growth Mechanism, Rebuilt

An independent design. Written to be read cold — no prior context required.
This document designs the growth system for Celestual from first principles. It was written after reading the two prior mechanism documents (the Hooked/STEPPS-derived design and the STEPPS audit) with an explicit mandate to discard anything wrong rather than polish it. Roughly half of the prior design is discarded here, and the reasons are stated specifically. Every design decision carries its reasoning and its source. The document ends with honest odds and an unsoftened list of failure modes.
The one fixed constraint, taken as given: Celestual is a double-blind mutual-reveal product. You enter someone's Instagram handle. They are never notified. If and only if they independently enter yours, both of you learn of the match at the same instant. If it is never mutual, nothing is ever revealed to anyone. Everything else below is open and was treated as open.

## Part 0 · Verdict on the prior documents

Before the new design, the specific disagreements, so you know exactly where this departs and why. What the prior documents got right and this design keeps: the campus-density thesis (density is the mechanic, so saturate one closed community at a time); the 100-person floor on any visible count; the absolute ban on individual "someone entered you" notifications; the high-arousal emotional register for content (Berger & Milkman's finding that anticipation spreads and melancholy doesn't); the observation that every match is a brand-inseparable story; the ban on institutional/workplace distribution; content as the reflex-installer. These survive because they are grounded in either hard evidence or hard legal precedent.
What this design rejects, with the argument in one line each (full arguments appear where each topic is treated):
1. The governing equation is wrong. The prior design says Celestual's fate is its viral coefficient K. K-factor math assumes users are interchangeable — any new user is worth the same as any other. For Celestual that is false: a new user in Ohio does nothing for a sender in Seoul. The value of growth is entirely graph-local. The governing metric is not K but ping resolution rate: the fraction of pings whose target is (or becomes) present on the platform. Ten thousand users spread thin are worth less than eight hundred on one campus. This reframe changes what you optimize, what you measure, and what "growth" even means. (Source: the network-clustering literature — Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations on local adoption clusters; Andrew Chen's The Cold Start Problem on "atomic networks"; Schelling's critical-mass models.)
2. The referral-currency economy (join = +1 free ping, uncapped) rewards the wrong thing with a worthless currency. Pings are not naturally scarce to a user. Most people have one to three humans they would ever enter — the scarce thing is the person, not the ping. An uncapped ping supply has near-zero marginal value to almost everyone, so as a referral reward it is weak; and where it isn't weak, it attracts exactly the spammy scanning behavior you don't want. The prior document half-noticed this (its own "attack" on Layer 1) and patched it instead of pulling the thread. The stronger, honest motivator is already sitting inside the product and costs nothing: the sender's own ping cannot resolve unless the target joins. Self-interest, truthfully stated, beats a token economy. This becomes Loop A below.
3. The $4.99/month subscription contradicts the company's own thesis. The vision document correctly frames Celestual as pest control — episodic, on-demand, a few moments a year. Pest control does not sell subscriptions to students; it charges per job. A recurring charge on a product used episodically produces churn, chargebacks, and resentment, and the anchor-and-decoy paywall theater ($2.99 anchor vs $4.99/mo target) optimizes revenue extraction at precisely the stage where the only thing that matters is density. Recommendation in Part 3: essentially don't monetize during the density-building phase, and when you do, charge one-time, never recurring.
4. The "secret society" share framing manufactures a promise the product can't keep. "You're being let in" earns a click, yes — and then the door opens onto a single text field. The gap between the mystique of the invitation and the thinness of what's inside produces a disappointment spike, and disappointed arrivals don't produce second-generation shares. The framing that survives contact with the actual product is the one the STEPPS audit itself identified and then underused: practical value — "there is a way to find out, with zero risk." Useful advice is the most deniable thing a person can transmit (recommending a tool confesses nothing), and it is true at every density including a community of one.
5. Designing for variable-reward chain-checking contradicts the episodic thesis. The prior design proudly makes "checking your chain" the compulsive pull, importing the slot-machine logic of Eyal and Skinner. But the vision document's best idea is that Celestual doesn't need daily engagement — it needs to be the reflex when the feeling hits. Engineering compulsive checking into an episodic product adds ethical surface area and build cost to purchase an engagement metric the business model doesn't require. Cut entirely.
6. User-managed named chains should be demoted, which saves Ace real build time. Chains as a user-facing product object require the user to understand a new concept ("what is a chain?"), require naming/moderation/dedup infrastructure, and their actual functions — community-level social proof and rollout telemetry — are preserved by something much simpler: automatic community counters (Part 2, section 5) plus the per-person share card (Loop B). Kill the chain as a thing users create and manage. Keep the counter and the pressure-gauge telemetry.
One more departure that isn't a rejection but a re-centering: the prior documents treat "the share" as a broadcast problem — how do we get users to spray a link. The design below treats the primary share as a targeting problem with a deniability constraint: every ping creates one highly motivated recruiter (the sender) aiming at one specific human (the target), and the whole game is giving that recruiter safe ammunition. Broadcast mechanics exist here too, but they are the cover for the targeted delivery, not the engine.

## Part 1 · The two structural facts everything follows from

### Fact 1: Every ping manufactures a motivated recruiter with a named target. The moment a user enters @someone who isn't on the platform, that user acquires a concrete, self-interested reason to get one specific person to join — a reason no other consumer product has to fabricate with referral bonuses. LinkedIn, WhatsApp, and every contacts-graph network grew on exactly this asymmetry: the product becomes more valuable to me when the specific people I care about join (Chen, The Cold Start Problem). Celestual has the strongest version of this motive in consumer software — the product is worthless to the sender until their person arrives — and the prior design barely uses it, because it collides with the privacy constraint: the sender cannot say "join, because I entered you" without destroying the double-blind and walking into the FTC's NGL precedent. So the central design problem of this entire document is:
How does a sender cause their target to join, through a channel that cannot implicate the sender?
Everything in Part 2 is the answer to that question. The answer has three parts: give the sender a deniable artifact to put where the target will see it (Loop B), surround every individual share with so much ambient sharing that no single share carries signal — herd cover (Loop C and the counters), and run a content engine so the target may arrive with no nudge from the sender at all (the existing content strategy, kept).
Fact 2: The product's honest promise is probabilistic silence. For most users, most of the time, the truthful experience of Celestual is: you entered someone, and then nothing happened, possibly forever. This is the product working as designed — silence is the safety — but it is also the single largest growth and retention threat, and no amount of framing removes it. The most instructive real-world comparison makes the danger precise. Marriage Pact (Stanford, 2017, since run at dozens of campuses with hundreds of thousands of participants) is the closest thing to a proven comp for a campus-density, episodic, matching-event product — and it worked in large part because it guaranteed a payoff: fill out the survey and you will receive a name. Celestual's expected payoff per user is a fraction of that. This gap is real, it is structural, and Part 2 designs around it three ways (honest expectation-setting at the send moment, aggregate payoffs that arrive even when personal ones don't, and the renewal touchpoint) — but it cannot be designed away, and the odds in Part 5 price it in.
Hold these two facts and the whole mechanism follows.

## Part 2 · The mechanism

Six components. Each one: what it is, why, and the source of the reasoning.
### 1. Loop A — The recruiter loop (primary)

What it is. The moment a user places a ping, the product tells them the truth about its status. If the target is already a verified user: "standing — @them is reachable. If they ever enter you, you'll both know at the same moment." If the target is not on the platform: "waiting — @them isn't on Celestual yet. Your ping can only resolve if they join. They will never know you're the reason they heard about it — here's how people do it." And then the product hands the sender the deniable playbook: post your own card to your Story (Loop B), or let your community's presence grow (counters, campus windows), or do nothing and let content reach them.
Why. This is the moment of peak motivation — the user has just performed an emotionally expensive act and wants it to mean something — and the ask is perfectly aligned with their self-interest. The prior design placed a share prompt at this same moment but attached it to the wrong reward ("more pings unlock when you share"). Nobody at this moment wants more pings; they want this ping to have a chance. Telling them the truth about resolution probability converts them into a recruiter using no incentive budget at all, and it is honest by construction: every word of the prompt is factually true. (Sources: Fogg's behavior model for trigger-at-peak-motivation placement — the one piece of the Eyal stack worth keeping here; the contacts-graph growth pattern per Chen.)
A necessary sub-decision: revealing target status. Showing "reachable / not yet" leaks one bit about the target: platform membership. Two protections make this acceptable. First, membership is framed and experienced platform-wide as the receiver identity ("findable," "door open" — see Loop B), which is the ego-safe, even flattering, side of the product; disclosing that someone is findable is like disclosing that someone has DMs open. Second, anti-scanning architecture: status is shown only after a ping is placed, a user can hold only three standing pings at once (see component 4), and lookups are rate-limited. Enumerating the user base would cost slots, time, and identity. Any Instagram handle owner — user or not — can also verify ownership and opt out permanently, making their handle un-pingable and un-storable (this matters legally too; see Part 6).
### 2. Loop B — The open-door card (the deniable artifact, and the distribution channel)

What it is. Every user gets a personal page — celestual.us/@their.handle — and a beautiful, brand-native Story card to post: deep navy, the single warm star, and copy in the receiver's voice. Something like: "if there's something you never said to me, there's now a safe place to say it. I'll never know unless it's mutual." Posting the card to your Instagram Story is the product's one public act. A viewer who taps through lands on that person's page, where the ping field is prefilled with the poster's handle — two taps from Story to placed ping — and from there the new user gets their own card.
Why this is the load-bearing share object. Three reasons, in descending order of importance.
It solves the humiliation tax by inversion, not by camouflage. The prior documents correctly diagnosed that "I'm thinking about someone I can't have" is exposure, and then tried to hide it inside Trojan-horse objects (secret societies, fun facts). The cleaner solution is to notice that every user has two identities on this platform — sender and receiver — and the receiver identity is flattering. "I am reachable; shoot your shot; say the unsaid thing to me" is an invitation and a mild flex. It confesses nothing. So the public face of Celestual is always, universally, the receiver face. Nobody ever shares as a sender; everybody shares as an open door. (Source: this is Berger's Social Currency principle satisfied properly — the share makes the sharer look desired, the strongest currency available to this product — rather than merely not-embarrassing.)
It is the sender's deniable delivery mechanism. Here is the quiet heart of the design. A sender whose target hasn't joined posts their own open-door card to their Story. The target — a follower — sees it, gets curious, joins. To the world, and to the target, the sender posted a generic flex to all their followers. Only the sender knows the card was aimed. The same artifact serves the genuinely open user and the covertly targeting one, and they are indistinguishable — which is precisely what makes it safe for both. This is the answer to the central design problem in Part 1.
It is a proven distribution channel, de-frauded. The Story-link loop — post a personal link, followers tap through, convert, post their own — is the exact mechanism that carried NGL and Sendit to tens of millions of installs on Instagram. It is the single most validated organic loop that exists on this specific platform for this specific demographic. NGL poisoned it with fake "someone sent you a message" notifications and got a $5M FTC order and a ban on serving minors; the loop itself was never the crime. Celestual runs the honest version: nothing on the card or the landing page ever implies any specific person has entered anyone. (Sources: NGL/Sendit growth history; FTC v. NGL Labs, 2024.)
The register risk, named. NGL's loop worked partly because it was juvenile — low-stakes, spammy, fine to post ironically. Celestual's card asks for a sincerer post, which is a higher bar. The mitigation is that the card must be genuinely beautiful and genuinely cool — a brand object people are proud to have on their Story, in the product's quiet literary voice, never pleading. Whether the mature register clears the bar on Stories is one of the honest unknowns priced into Part 5. If the sincere version stalls, the fallback register is deadpan/wry (the card copy can A/B toward irony without touching the mechanism).
### 3. Loop C — Assurance-contract campus openings (now optional)

**Repositioned (2026-07-05): Celestual no longer gates access behind a campus threshold.** The product is globally open — anyone can place a ping the moment they arrive (§2.5). The assurance-contract campus window below is retained as an *optional* launch accelerant an ambassador can run to open a whole school on one day, not as the door everyone must wait behind. Its old job — being the density incentive — is now carried by the fixed-100 community stats (§2.5), which pull members in without ever locking anyone out. Read the rest of this section as "a tool available for a coordinated campus push," not "the way people get in."

What it is. A campus doesn't have to dribble onto Celestual; it can open all at once. The playbook per campus: the ambassador seeds it, posters and Fizz posts carry one message — "Celestual opens at [school] when 300 people are in. Current count: 214." — and a campus page shows a live progress meter toward the threshold. Preregistration is the full signup (verify your handle), so the moment the threshold trips, the campus goes live with real density on day one, everyone is notified at once, and the first pings land into a populated network. The threshold is set as a fraction of the student body — start around 10–15% for a 1,500–2,500 LAC, i.e. roughly 200–350 people, and tune with data; the right number is wherever a median user's "person" has a real chance of being present.
Why. Three independent lines of reasoning converge on this design.
Critical-mass theory says don't open below threshold. A density-dependent product launched thin delivers its worst possible experience (universal silence) to its earliest, loudest users. Schelling's tipping models and Granovetter's threshold models both formalize the same intuition: participation cascades once enough others participate, and stalls permanently below that point. The assurance-contract structure — nothing starts until N commit, so no early joiner ever experiences the empty room — is the standard mechanism-design solution to exactly this problem (Bagnoli & Lipman, 1989; Kickstarter is the folk version). You are not launching a product at a campus; you are running an assurance contract on a campus.
The threshold itself is the persuasion. A visible count climbing toward a communal unlock is social proof (people join what their people are joining), urgency (it's happening with or without you), and a Public artifact (the poster's QR points at the meter, the meter gets screenshotted into group chats) — all in one number, all factually true. This does honestly what the "secret society" framing did theatrically.
Preregistration is herd cover. Joining a live confession platform signals "I have someone." Preregistering for a campus-wide event that hasn't opened signals nothing — you're joining the event, like signing the Marriage Pact survey. Marriage Pact's campus penetration (majorities of some undergraduate classes) proves that synchronized, deadline-shaped, whole-campus participation formats clear the embarrassment bar that ambient app adoption doesn't. Celestual borrows the format (a windowed communal moment) while keeping its own mechanic inside it. (Source: Marriage Pact, Stanford 2017–present; Rogers on observability accelerating adoption within bounded communities.)
The week-one aggregate reveal. Because most individuals will personally experience silence (Fact 2), the opening must deliver a collective payoff on a known date: one week after opening, the campus page and an email to all participants publish the aggregates — "Reed, week one: 1,940 pings placed. 63 mutual matches." No individuals, ever; numbers only, and only above the 100-floor. This is the visible fire that keeps the smoke interesting: even a user whose own ping sits silent learns that the machine works, here, on people they walk past. It is also the story seed — 63 matches is 63 couples/reconciliations who each own a retellable, brand-inseparable story (the Stories strength the STEPPS audit correctly identified). Every number published must be exactly true; the moment one is inflated, this becomes the NGL pattern (see Part 6).
### 4. The three-slot rule and the sixty-day ping

What it is. A user may hold at most three standing pings at once, and each ping stands for sixty days, silently expiring unless renewed. Renewal is free, unlimited, and one tap — triggered by an email a few days before lapse: "your ping for @them lapses Friday. still feel it? keep it standing." When a ping expires unrenewed, its record is purged.
Why the cap. Scarcity here is not a monetization trick; it is what makes the gesture mean something. A product where you can enter forty people is a scanning tool and a joke; a product where you hold three is a place for the people who actually occupy your head. The cap enforces sincerity (which protects the receiving side's trust in what a match means), blocks enumeration/scanning (you cannot sweep a campus three handles at a time), and keeps the match signal honest — when it's mutual, both people know the other spent a scarce slot. Wanting a fourth slot is also the only natural place money ever fits into this product (Part 3), and a cap you can pay to extend never blocks anyone: you can always retire a standing ping to free the slot. (Source: costly-signaling logic — a signal is credible in proportion to what it costs to send; three-of-three is a cost, unlimited is noise.)
Why the expiry. Four birds, one design. It creates the recurring trigger the STEPPS audit correctly flagged as Celestual's most dangerous missing piece — but attaches it to a real event in the user's own emotional life ("do you still feel it?") rather than a manufactured weekly ritual, so it re-triggers exactly the people for whom the feeling is still alive and gracefully releases the rest. It is a re-recruitment moment (a renewal email can honestly note the target still hasn't joined, and re-offer the playbook). It is privacy hygiene: unresolved longing data self-destructs instead of accumulating into a toxic archive (see Part 6 — this materially shrinks the breach and the GDPR/PIPA surface). And it gives closure a shape: letting a ping lapse is a small, private act of moving on, which is on-thesis for a resolution product. Note what the renewal email is not: it is about the user's own action only, never about the target's activity — that line is load-bearing legally.
### 5. Communities and community stats (the fixed-100 model)

What it is. At signup, a user optionally tags the real communities they belong to — their school, plus up to two scenes ("Korean intl students," "NYU Class of 2028"). Placing a ping never depends on any of this: **Celestual is open to everyone, everywhere, from day one** — a friend group of five can be on it together the moment they arrive, and pings between them work immediately. What a community's size gates is not access but a **reward**: at a fixed **100 members**, that community's **weekly stats open** — how many people in it got a mutual match this week, the most common reason people entered someone (the intent lines), pings placed, who joined. Below 100 the community is "gathering": its member count stays hidden (the 100-floor — small counts both feel empty and de-anonymize) and the stats are locked behind a preview that names exactly what opens at 100. The counter and the `/c/<slug>` link exist from the first member; only the numbers wait.

Why a fixed 100, and why stats rather than access. The prior design gated a whole campus behind a *variable* threshold set as a fraction of the student body (§3 below) — celestual "opened" only once 10–15% preregistered. That has two fatal problems once the product goes global. First, it locks people out: the friend group of five who want in together are told to wait for a number they can't move, which is the opposite of "start local and intimate." Second, and decisively, **there is no gauge of a community's true size** when you are marketing to the entire world — an Instagram scene, a diaspora, a fandom has no known denominator, so no "appropriate" per-community threshold can be computed at all. A single fixed number sidesteps both: it never blocks anyone (pings always work), and 100 is small enough that any real community can reach it yet large enough to keep every published number above the de-anonymization floor. The stats are the pull — a member watching their world sit just under 100, next to another world's live weekly matches, has a concrete, self-interested reason to bring more of their people in. This is the recruiter motive (Fact 1) applied to the community instead of the individual, and it keeps the intimate, "your scene is here" feeling without ever being a gate.

Why this still replaces chains. Same social proof, same rollout telemetry (counter velocity is the pressure gauge), no user-facing concept to explain, no chain-creation UI, no naming moderation, no ownership questions. The 100-floor is kept exactly as before — it now governs the stats unlock as well as every displayed count. Two floors compose: a community's member count and its weekly aggregates publish only at 100+, and inside those, a match count publishes only at 10+ (§2.7's match-count floor), so a small week shows "matches open at ten" rather than a guessable number. Every number shown is exactly true or absent.
### 6. The content engine (kept, with its role sharpened)

The existing strategy — the DM series, street interviews, Vi's weekly structure, the high-arousal "held-breath possibility" register, crossposted to Reels/TikTok/Shorts — is correct and is not redesigned here. What this document adds is precision about its role in the machine: content is the third recruitment channel, the one requiring nothing from the sender. When content installs the reflex broadly, some fraction of targets arrive on their own, and every sender's ping quietly gains resolution probability without anyone acting. Content is also the only loop that works at zero density, which makes it the correct primary activity during the military window when campus windows can't be properly run. One integration point: match stories harvested (with enthusiastic consent) from the week-one reveals become the strongest possible content input — real, improbable, brand-inseparable narratives (Berger's Stories principle, already correctly identified as the product's structural advantage).
How the loops mesh
A person feels the feeling (installed by content, or organic) → places a ping → Loop A tells them the truth and hands them the playbook → they post their open-door card (Loop B), which is simultaneously their flex and their targeted delivery → viewers arrive two taps from a placed ping, becoming senders themselves → density rises, counters climb, campus meters fill → windows open, aggregate reveals fire, matches happen → matches become stories, stories become content, content installs the reflex in the next community. The engagement loop and the growth loop are the same loop — the prior document's best structural observation, achieved here without the token economy.

## Part 3 · Monetization

The recommendation is stark: do not monetize during the density phase. Keep Stripe plumbed and dormant. The reasoning, stated as an argument rather than an assertion:
The company's binding constraint is density, not revenue. Two founders in the military, a web-only product with trivial infrastructure cost, no salaries — the burn rounds to zero, so revenue solves no live problem. Meanwhile every dollar-shaped surface in the flow taxes the two behaviors that are the live problem: pings (each suppressed ping is a lost latent match, and matches are the story engine) and shares. A paywall at the send moment converts the product's most valuable act into its revenue extraction point — maximally efficient, maximally corrosive. The prior document's own pest-control framing argues against its own subscription: episodic products fit per-job pricing, and a $4.99/month recurring charge on something used a few times a year is a churn-and-chargeback machine that also quietly re-imports the retention anxiety the pest-control framing was invented to dissolve.
The "paid signals legitimacy and wards off the data-harvesting fear" argument deserves an answer because it's the best argument for charging: it's real but weak. Trust on a small campus comes from a warm named brand, a real ambassador, visible safety architecture, and truthful numbers — not from a Stripe checkout, which for plenty of students reads as less trustworthy, not more.
When money does enter (post-density, post-proof, plausibly post-discharge), the shape that fits the mechanism is already sitting in Part 2: the three-slot rule. One-time purchases only — a fourth slot, or "keep this ping standing indefinitely" (no sixty-day renewals) — priced as a small dignity-preserving one-off. This monetizes intensity of feeling rather than access to the core act, never blocks anyone (slots can always be freed by retiring a ping), requires no anchoring theater, and touches neither the share loop nor the first-ping experience. It will make less money than the subscription design in year one. It is the correct trade, and it is reversible later; a poisoned first impression on a 2,000-person campus is not.

## Part 4 · The screens

Described in words precisely enough to build from. Global visual language throughout: deep navy fields, generous empty space, the single warm orange star as the only accent, serif italics for the emotional voice and small quiet sans-serif for the mechanical voice. Every screen has exactly one primary action. The felt register everywhere is quiet, adult, certain — the 2am message, never the carnival.
Screen 1 · Cold landing (celestual.us). A visitor arrives from content or word of mouth. Near-black navy page, the small star centered high. The hero line. Beneath it, three short lines in small type, the practical-value promise stated flat: "enter their instagram handle. they never find out. unless they enter you too — then you both do, at the same moment." One button, warm orange, generous: "find out." Under the button, one line of small type doing the safety work: "no profiles. no browsing. nothing happens unless it's mutual." Nothing else — no navigation weight, no feature grid, no counters here (a cold visitor has no community context yet). What the visitor should feel: recognized (the hero line names a feeling they have), then safe (the mechanics read like a locked door), then curious. Why the copy leads with the ache and lands on the utility: high-arousal recognition earns the read (Berger & Milkman), but it's the zero-risk usefulness that a person can later repeat to a friend without confessing anything — the landing page is writing the word-of-mouth sentence for them.
Screen 2 · The send. One centered input with the @ prefix, placeholder "their handle." Above it, small and calm: "who is it?" Below the field, the optional intent row. This feature is kept exactly as designed; it is what widens the product beyond romance without forcing self-categorization, and revealing it only at match gives the match its narrative payload.) Have the visuals for the amount of pings left underneath all this. What the user should feel: weight, not friction — the three-slot line is doing deliberate emotional work, making the act feel like setting something down carefully rather than firing off a DM. Why one field and nothing else: the action must be executable inside a wave of courage before it passes (Fogg — ability high, trigger present, motivation fleeting).
Screen 3 · Placed — the recruiter screen (the most important screen in the product). Two states.
State A, target is reachable: the star, then: "it's standing." Sub-line: "@them is on celestual. if they ever enter you, you'll both know in the same moment. until then — silence, which is the point." One quiet secondary action: "post your door" (Loop B). The user should feel: the held breath. The copy explicitly dignifies the silence so the coming days of nothing are pre-framed as the product working, not the product being dead — this is the Fact-2 mitigation applied at the moment expectations form.
State B, target hasn't joined: the star, then, honestly: "it's waiting." Sub-line: "@them isn't on celestual yet. your ping can only resolve if they join — and they will never know you had anything to do with it." Then a short titled block, "how people do it," with three lines: "post your door to your story — it says nothing except that you're reachable" / "your worlds: korean intl students · 2,341 in" (when above the floor) / "or wait. this is spreading on its own." Primary button: "post your door." The user should feel: agency instead of helplessness, and total safety in acting. Why this screen exists: it is Loop A — the recruitment ask placed at peak motivation, powered by the truth, aimed by self-interest. Every sentence on it must remain literally true forever.
Screen 4 · Your pings (the status page). A quiet list, at most three rows. Each row: the handle, the state word (standing with a faint warm dot / waiting in cooler grey), days remaining of the sixty, and a small "let it go" affordance that retires the ping with one confirmation ("this frees the slot. nothing was ever revealed."). Near lapse, a row gains one line: "lapses in 4 days — keep it standing?" with a single tap renewal. No activity feed, no engagement bait, nothing that rewards checking — deliberately boring by design, because this product's integrity is that nothing happens here until everything does. What the user should feel: that their feelings are being held somewhere careful and unhurried.
Screen 5 · The open-door card and its landing. In-product, "post your door" shows the rendered Story card: navy, star, the user's handle small at the bottom, and the receiver-voice line in serif italic — "if there's something you never said to me, it's safe here now." — with the personal link. One button renders it to the user's camera roll sized for Stories, with the link ready to paste as the Story's link sticker. (Web-only reality: Instagram doesn't allow third-party direct-to-Story posting worth relying on; download-plus-link-sticker is the honest flow, and the card must be gorgeous enough to be worth the two steps.) When a viewer taps through, they land on celestual.us/@poster: the poster's handle at top, the line "@poster is reachable on celestual," a prefilled ping field ready to send to the poster in two taps, and beneath it, small: "someone else on your mind? enter anyone." What the visitor should feel: a small jolt — the door is specifically, personally open — followed by the private redirect to whoever they actually think about at 2am. Why the prefill: it collapses the distance from curiosity to core action to two taps, and the poster-directed ping is the single most likely first ping a viewer has (they tapped for a reason).
Screen · Your worlds (communities — the fixed-100 stats, §2.5). Reached from the status page and the account sheet. Each community the user has tagged is a card. Past 100 members: a "stats open" card carrying the live weekly readout, written as language rather than a dashboard — one hero number ("18 matched this week"), the most common reason as a real quote ("most said 'i miss you'"), and a quiet line of pings-placed and joined-this-week; a small live dot marks it fresh, and a "share this world" action hands over the `/c/` link. Under 100 members: a quieter "gathering" card that shows *no count* (the floor) and a locked preview naming exactly what opens at 100 — mutual matches, the most common reason, pings this week — with a "bring your people" share. One honest line sits over the whole screen: "everyone can ping from day one. at 100, a world's weekly stats open." What the user should feel: their scene is here and alive, the reward of reaching 100 is concrete and visible, and nothing about their own use was ever gated. In the sandbox this screen is seeded (NYU Class of 2028 open with live stats; others still gathering) and a "sandbox: new activity" control shows the weekly numbers updating live.
Screen 6 · The campus window (pre-opening — optional). Retained as an optional launch tool, not the access gate (§2.3). The school's page: name up top in the label style, then the meter — a thin horizontal line filling with warm light, the count in large numerals: "214 of 300." Above it: "celestual opens at reed when 300 are in." Below: "preregistering reveals nothing about you. it just opens the door for everyone at once." One button: "count me in" — which runs full signup and handle verification so opening day has real, verified density. Small type at the bottom: "when it opens, everyone finds out together." What a visitor should feel: that something is coming to their whole world with or without them, and that joining costs nothing socially — event participation, not confession. The poster on the dining-hall wall carries only the school name, the date-less threshold line, and a QR to this page; the meter is the campaign.
Screen 7 · Open, and the week-one reveal. At threshold, every preregistrant gets the email — subject: "reed is open." — and the campus page flips to: "it's open. 300 of your campus is in — and counting." with the send field right there. Seven days later, the reveal, on the page and by email: "reed, week one: 1,940 pings placed. 63 mutual matches. every one of them found out something true." Numbers only, exactly accurate, never individuals, never below the 100-floor. What the community should feel: the machine is real, here, working on people they know — the collective payoff that carries the majority whose personal pings sit silent.
Screen 8 · The match. Both parties, same moment, by email (subject quiet and unmistakable: "celestual: it's mutual.") linking to this screen. The star, slightly larger than anywhere else in the product — the one place the brand permits brightness. Serif italic: "it's mutual." Then: "you entered @them. @them entered you. this only ever happens when it's real on both sides." If either attached an intent, revealed now, quoted in a bordered block: they said: "i never got to say something." One button: "go say it" — deep-linking to the Instagram DM thread — and beneath it, the product's exit line, small: "the rest is yours. celestual's part is done." No share button, no confetti, no screenshot-bait artifact: the story travels by telling (which forces the brand to travel with it, per Berger's Trojan-horse test), and the absence of a gotcha artifact is the anti-humiliation architecture. What both people should feel: awe, and the sensation that the product stepped back at exactly the right moment — which is itself the detail people retell.
Screen 9 · The fourth slot (dormant, for later). Only ever reachable when a user with three standing pings tries to place a fourth. Copy: "you're holding three. you can let one go — or hold a fourth." Two equal doors: "let one go" (routes to Screen 4) and, when monetization wakes, "hold a fourth — $X, once." Never a subscription, never at the first ping, never in the share loop. Until monetization wakes, the screen simply shows the first door.

## Part 5 · Honest odds

Confidence stated per component, with the basis labeled grounded (comparable real-world cases exist) or reasoned (coherent argument, no direct proof). Do not average these into one number; the design fails at its weakest live link.
That the Story-card loop can distribute at meaningful scale — grounded, high confidence in the channel, genuine uncertainty on the register. The link-in-Story loop carried NGL and Sendit to tens of millions of installs with this demographic on this platform; that the pipe works is about as proven as anything in consumer growth. Whether Celestual's sincere, adult card clears the posting bar the way NGL's ironic junk did is unproven and is my single biggest mechanism-level uncertainty. Call it 40–60% that the sincere register works as designed, with the wry-register fallback recoverable if it doesn't.
That assurance-contract campus windows can hit threshold — grounded, moderate confidence. Marriage Pact proves whole-campus synchronized participation formats work at LACs and beyond; assurance contracts are proven mechanism design; posters-plus-Fizz-plus-ambassador is a known playbook. But Marriage Pact guarantees every participant a payoff and Celestual doesn't — a real structural discount — and your first windows will be run remotely from Korea through ambassadors, a further discount on the ground-energy this format feeds on. Per-campus odds of hitting a 10–15% threshold with a good ambassador: I'd say 25–40%. Across four or five sequenced attempts, the odds that at least one campus opens properly are decent — better than even — and one true opening is the proof the whole thesis needs.
That an opened campus produces a self-sustaining story culture — reasoned, low-to-moderate confidence. This hinges on the match rate, the number nobody can know in advance: what fraction of pings resolve mutual inside a dense community? If week one at a 300-person opening produces dozens of matches, the reveal lands, stories circulate, the reflex installs. If it produces four, the aggregate reveal embarrasses rather than ignites. There is no comp for this number. It is the experiment the first opening exists to run, and I will not pretend a confidence level on it.
That the mechanic alone is inherently viral — grounded, and the evidence is against it. This deserves blunt stating: Facebook shipped this exact mechanic — Secret Crush, inside Facebook Dating, 2019 — with effectively infinite density and zero cold-start problem, and it made no cultural dent. Bang With Friends (2013) spiked on the same double-blind idea and died of brand cringe. The mechanic is necessary but has never once been sufficient; everything rides on the wrapper — the moment-making (windows, reveals), the brand register, the content engine. Anyone who tells you "the mechanic sells itself" is wrong on the record.
That Celestual becomes a durable multi-year company — reasoned, low confidence, and I'll put a number on it: 5–10% from where you stand. That is not an insult; it is roughly the honest base rate for pre-traction consumer social with a two-person team, adjusted up for an unusually coherent thesis and near-zero burn, and down for the founders being non-deployable until April 2027 and for the non-romantic use cases — the entire long-term TAM story — currently having zero behavioral evidence behind them. The military window is correctly framed in your own vision doc: it buys understanding, not traction. The realistic good outcome of the next eighteen months is one opened campus, one week-one reveal with real numbers, and a content engine with compounding reach — which together would move that 5–10% substantially.

## Part 6 · Failure modes and risks, unsoftened

I am not a lawyer; these are named risks, not legal advice. Ordered roughly by expected damage.
1. The silence problem is the most likely killer. Most users' complete experience: enter someone, hear nothing, forever. The design mitigates (expectation-framing at Screen 3, aggregate reveals, renewal touchpoints, episodic positioning) but cannot cure. The observable failure signature: strong opening-week numbers, then the campus goes quiet, week-one reveal is the peak, no second campus ignites from the first's stories. Watch ping resolution rate and match rate per opened campus as the numbers that decide everything; if match rate inside a properly dense community is structurally tiny, the product thesis — not the growth design — is what's wrong.
2. The forbidden lever will tempt you, by name, when growth stalls. Someday a stalled week will make "notify people that someone entered them" or "imply activity to lapsed users" or "pad a counter" look like a small compromise. That is the NGL pattern; the FTC fined it $5M, banned it from serving minors, and made it the category's cautionary tale. Every counter, meter, and reveal number in this design is legal because it is true; truth is the entire margin. Pre-commit now, in writing, in the founders' operating docs: no individual implication, no inflated numbers, ever, under any growth pressure. (Your vision doc already holds this line; I'm flagging that the pressure to cross it will arrive dressed as a reasonable experiment.)
3. Shadow data on non-users is a real GDPR/PIPA exposure the prior documents never mention. When a user pings @someone who never joins, you are storing a third party's personal data (an Instagram handle is identifiable data) without their knowledge, attached to the most sensitive inference imaginable — that someone longs for them. Mitigations, all cheap, all should be day-one: store unmatched target handles salted-and-hashed (match by hash; the sender's own device/account view is the only place the plaintext renders), purge on the sixty-day expiry (the expiry is doing legal work, not just trigger work), and run a public opt-out page where any handle owner can verify and become permanently un-pingable. Korea's PIPA applies to you personally while you operate from Korea; EU users bring GDPR the moment a chain of Korean intl students crosses into Europe, which it will.
4. A breach of this database is company-ending and personally harmful to users. You are building a map of unrequited longing, indexed by real identities. A leak doesn't embarrass the company; it outs feelings people chose never to reveal — the exact harm the product promises to make impossible. Encrypt at rest, minimize retention (expiry again), log access, and treat the unmatched-ping table as the crown jewels. This also argues for never storing more than the mechanism strictly needs.
5. Impersonation and false-claim matches are a non-negotiable pre-launch fix. If handle ownership isn't verified before a match can fire, someone claims @your-ex's handle, enters you back, and manufactures a fake "it's mutual" — the cruelest possible failure, and one screenshot of it ends a campus. No match fires to an unverified claimant, period. The interim bio-code verification is acceptable; the OAuth path via a borrowed Facebook developer account is its own risk (below).
6. Meta platform risk sits under the whole company. Celestual is Instagram-handle-native with zero API rights, and the planned OAuth route through a friend's established Facebook developer account is a ToS violation waiting for an enforcement pass — if Meta pulls it, verification breaks in production. Treat the borrowed account as scaffolding with a planned replacement (a legitimate Meta app review under the company's own entity, post-discharge), and keep the bio-verification path maintained forever as the fallback. Accept, strategically, that Meta could clone the mechanic (they already did once, quietly — Secret Crush); your only moats are density-in-communities and brand, which is another argument for winning whole campuses rather than scattering.
7. Minors will hit the Story loop no matter what. Cards posted to Stories reach whoever follows the poster, including high schoolers, and the FTC's NGL order shows regulators treat this category plus minors as a priority target. Hard age gate at signup, college-and-up marketing only (already your policy), fast purge on suspected-minor accounts, and never let an ambassador near a high school for any reason. This is a place to be boringly conservative.
8. Stalking and harassment — mostly defused by the architecture, honestly. The double-blind is genuinely protective: a ping never notifies, so the product cannot be used to contact someone who cut contact — silence in, silence out. Three residual edges: the "reconnection" positioning flirts with encouraging outreach toward people who ended contact deliberately (keep the copy on resolution, never pursuit); a manipulative ex who matches gains a legitimized re-entry (the block list must be architectural — a blocked handle can never match you, and blocking must survive the blocker's own curiosity); and the opt-out page (risk 3) doubles as the escape hatch for anyone who wants out of the system entirely, user or not.
9. The receiver-framing has a failure mode of its own. If posting your open-door card comes to read as thirsty rather than quietly confident, Loop B dies of cringe — the Bang With Friends death. This is a brand-register problem, which means it's a content and copy problem, which means it's testable cheaply before any campus window: seed the card with a handful of the right people (Lauren's and Vi's orbits are exactly this test bed) and watch whether it gets posted sincerely, ironically, or not at all. Run that test before Ace builds anything downstream of it.
10. The two-founders-in-uniform constraint is a growth-design input, not just a biography fact. Campus windows feed on ground energy you cannot supply until April 2027, and ambassadors are borrowed, fragile assets. The honest sequencing: military window = content engine + product hardening (verification, safety, opt-out, hashing) + at most one or two remote campus attempts treated explicitly as calibration, with the real opening offensive aimed at fall 2027 — the first semester you can stand on a campus yourself. Budget your own expectations to that timeline so a quiet 2026 doesn't read as failure; per your own doc, it isn't.

### The one-paragraph version for Ace

Celestual grows on three honest loops. Loop A: the moment someone places a ping, tell them the truth — their answer only becomes possible if their person joins — and hand them safe ways to make that happen. Loop B: the only public act on the platform is the receiver-side flex: a beautiful personal Story card ("if there's something you never said to me, it's safe here"), which doubles as the sender's deniable way to reach their target, and which lands viewers two taps from their own first ping. Loop C (now optional): a campus *can* open all at once, Marriage-Pact-style, via an assurance-contract preregistration with a truthful week-one aggregate reveal — but this is a launch accelerant, not a gate. Celestual is globally open: anyone can place a ping the day they arrive. Density is instead pulled by communities — a person tags the real scenes they're in, and at a fixed 100 members that community's weekly stats open (matches, the most common reason, pings placed), which rewards bringing more people in without ever locking anyone out. Pings are capped at three standing and expire in sixty days unless renewed (sincerity, safety, privacy, and the product's recurring trigger, in one rule). Chains are replaced by automatic community counters, hidden under 100. No money until density is proven; when it comes, it's a one-time fourth slot, never a subscription. Everything shown to any user is literally true, always — that truth is the legal and ethical margin the entire design lives inside.
